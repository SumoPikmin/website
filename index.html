Projects

    Master Thesis: Understanding and Mitigating Hallucinations in Large Language Models (LLMs) via Multi-Agent-Debate
    (October 2025 – May 2026)

    During my Master’s thesis at Fraunhofer IKS and TU Darmstadt, I designed and developed a collaborative multi-agent framework aimed at enhancing the factual reliability and robustness of LLMs. To address model interpretability, I engineered a detection mechanism utilizing classification probes to analyze internal model activations, allowing for the identification of latent errors during the inference process. I further architected a sophisticated orchestration workflow where autonomous agents engage in iterative debate and self-reflection to verify and refine outputs, significantly improving deterministic performance. Validated within a complex Safety Requirements Engineering context, this framework demonstrated substantial gains in consistency and factual accuracy, providing a scalable solution for deploying reliable LLMs in safety-critical environments.
    Bachelor Thesis: Evaluating Cultural Diversity in Text-to-Image Models
    (June 2024 – December 2024)

    During my Bachelor's thesis, I developed a large-scale benchmark to evaluate cultural biases in state-of-the-art Text-to-Image models. By leveraging a multilingual dataset spanning 2500 concepts in five languages and over 2.3 million compositional prompts, I designed an innovative evaluation pipeline using advanced Visual Question Answering models to accurately measure text-image alignment. This research revealed a significant performance gap of over 10% when generating content related to non-Western cultures, highlighting systemic cultural and multilingual biases in current generative AI systems. The project was awarded an outstanding grade of 1.0 (A+). View on GitHub.
    Multimodal AI Project: Semantic Detection of Fake Images
    (April 2024 – July 2024)

    As part of the Multimodal Artificial Intelligence course at TU Darmstadt, I co-developed a hybrid system to detect AI-generated images using semantic understanding instead of visual artifacts. By combining image captioning, NLP, and similarity ranking, the model classifies images based on how well their captions align with typical concepts. Trained on curated real and synthetic image sets, it achieved nearly 78% accuracy across diverse categories like animals and vehicles. The project also explored explainability and LLM input constraints, laying groundwork for future improvements in multimodal fake media detection. View on GitHub

    Traffic Management System: Incident Module
    (October 2023 – March 2024)

    As part of a university software project at the Institute for Railway Systems, TU Darmstadt, I created a simulation and GUI-based Incident Management Module for the Train Management System using Python and Tkinter. This tool allowed interactive configuration and management of over 25 railway incident scenarios, supporting training and operational planning. I built a real-time simulation environment featuring incident visualization and implemented backend logic to execute scenarios and analyze system responses. By applying agile methodologies including Scrum and conducting thorough usability testing, the module achieved a 30% improvement in task efficiency, demonstrating both technical and practical impact.
    Sentiment Classifier for Tweets using a Neural Network
    (December 2021 - February 2022)

    As part of a team in the Foundations of Language Technology course, I contributed to developing a simple feedforward neural network to classify tweets into positive, negative, or other sentiments. The project involved extensive preprocessing including stopword removal, lemmatization, stemming, and filtering of noisy text data. Using TensorFlow and NLTK, we trained and evaluated the model on a real-world multilingual tweet dataset, addressing challenges such as severe class imbalance. This collaborative work laid a foundation for further exploration into deep learning for sentiment analysis and text classification. View on Github.
